District Health Information System (DHIS)

DHIS (District Health Information System)

The District Health Information System (DHIS) falls into the category (of data collection for information management and decision-making). At the district, regional, and provincial levels, DHIS was used to collect and examine monthly data reports.

District Health Information Systems (DHIS) is used in countries to record data that is routinely gathered from all public health institutions.

It was created so that national-level analyses of health care delivery, service demand projections for use in strategic planning, and practitioner assessments could make better use of routinely gathered data from throughout a country's public health institutions (Garrib et al., 2008).

The system's key objectives were to set up a health center-based reporting database, develop local and national reporting guidelines for health centers, and link service delivery and other health system input databases (Manya et al., 2012).

The majority of the world's population uses the free and open-source software known as district health information system (DHIS). It is essential to the success of the Health Information Systems Programme (HISP). Here is a rundown of what happened during HISP's infancy that paved the way for DHIS to be created:. South Africa's healthcare system is notoriously unequal since it was left to the country's new democratic government after apartheid ended in 1994. The health care system needed to be rebuilt, therefore the new democratic administration set about doing just that. Thus, in 1995, a proposal was made to launch a pilot program to create a district-wide health and management information system.

DHIS was originally based on the Microsoft Office Access platform. As a standalone database management system, it was deemed beneficial. Several nations have employed DHIS to gather and analyze data on a monthly basis, at the municipal, regional, and provincial levels. In these instances, the DHIS was installed locally on a variety of computers around the country, each with its own local copy of the data.
